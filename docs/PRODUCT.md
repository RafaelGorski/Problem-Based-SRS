# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Software engineers working on brownfield projects. They inherit existing codebases, need to understand the system before modifying it, and want to trace how their next feature impacts the final customer. They reach for this methodology when a stakeholder says "build X" and they need to answer "why X, and what does X actually solve?" before writing code. Their context: mid-sprint, staring at a codebase they didn't write, trying to figure out what matters.

## Product Purpose

Problem-Based SRS is an AI-guided requirements engineering methodology. It exists because the #1 cause of software project failures is building what stakeholders asked for instead of what they need. The README and project webpage are the primary surfaces: they must convince a skeptical engineer in 30 seconds that this methodology is worth their time. Success looks like an engineer installing the skill and running their first `/problem-based-srs` session within 5 minutes of landing on the page.

## Brand Personality

Rational, grounded, precise. The voice of a senior engineer who has seen too many wasted sprints and now insists on evidence before code. Not academic, not preachy. Direct, specific, confident. Every claim backed by structure, not superlatives. The brand earns trust by showing the methodology works, not by telling you it's great.

## Anti-references

- Bootstrap-template layouts: generic card grids, purple gradients, rounded-pill buttons with no voice
- CRUD/list catalog interfaces: tables of content dressed as landing pages
- Old-school search/directory layouts: dense, undifferentiated, no hierarchy
- Generic SaaS marketing pages: hero-metric templates, "supercharge your workflow" copy, identical testimonial cards
- Any layout that looks like it shipped from a template with color swaps

## Design Principles

1. **Show the structure, not the pitch.** The methodology has a clear CP → CN → FR chain. Let the information architecture mirror it. The page should feel like the methodology itself: layered, traceable, purposeful.
2. **Earned confidence.** No buzzwords, no superlatives. Let the example speak: show the before/after of a real requirements session. Confidence comes from demonstrated competence.
3. **Engineer's time is sacred.** Dense where it needs to be, spacious where it helps. No decorative filler. Every section either teaches something or moves the engineer toward installation.
4. **Fluid and intentional.** Layout and typography should feel considered, not templated. Asymmetry where it creates emphasis. Rhythm through varied spacing. The page should reward scrolling.
5. **Practice what we preach.** A methodology about solving real problems should itself solve the reader's real problem: "Should I use this?" Answer it fast, answer it honestly.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA compliance. All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background. Reduced-motion alternatives for all animations. Color is never the sole indicator of meaning. Keyboard navigation works throughout. Semantic HTML structure for screen readers.
